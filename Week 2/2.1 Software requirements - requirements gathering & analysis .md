# Week 2 — Lesson 1: Software Requirements — Requirements Gathering & Analysis

## Lesson Overview

Hey, welcome to this lesson! Here is the simple version of what you are about to learn: before anyone writes a single line of code for a software system, someone has to figure out exactly what that software needs to do. This lesson is all about that very first step, called **requirements gathering and analysis** — finding out what the customer wants, and then double-checking that what you found out actually makes sense. Do not worry if this sounds vague right now; think of it like ordering food at a restaurant. Before the chef cooks anything, a waiter has to come to your table, ask what you want, write it down correctly, and check with you that they got it right. If the waiter gets it wrong, you get the wrong dish, you send it back, and everyone wastes time and money. Software works exactly the same way, and by the end of this lesson, you will know why skipping this step is one of the most expensive mistakes a software team can make.

## Concept 1: Importance of Requirements Identification

### Simple Definition
**Requirements identification** is the process of figuring out and writing down exactly what a software system needs to do, before anyone starts building it. Think of it as creating a shopping list before you go to the supermarket, so you know exactly what to buy instead of guessing.

### Why It Exists
Requirements identification exists because, in the past, many software teams skipped this step or rushed through it. Developers would just start building software based on their own guess of what the customer wanted. This caused a huge problem: the developer's idea of the software was often completely different from what the customer actually had in mind. Requirements identification was created as a formal step to stop developers from guessing and to make sure everyone agrees on what is being built before any building happens.

### Why It Matters to You
You need to understand this because it directly affects how much time, money, and stress goes into any software project you ever work on. If you ignore this step, here is what breaks: you will build features nobody asked for, you will miss features people actually needed, and you will have to redo large chunks of work later. Every time you redo work, it costs extra money and extra time, and the project gets delayed. In short, skipping this step now always costs you more later.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Imagine you ask a friend to buy you a "phone charger" from the store but you do not tell them which brand or which cable type you need. Your friend comes back with a charger, but it does not fit your phone at all. Now you have to send them back to the store, which wastes time and maybe even money if the wrong charger cannot be returned. This is exactly what happens in software when requirements are not clearly identified — the developer builds something, but it does not "fit" what the customer actually needed, so it has to be redone. Just like you needed to clearly tell your friend the exact brand and cable type, software teams need to clearly identify and write down requirements before building anything.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. The team first asks the customer directly: what do you want this software to do? Remember, the customer is not always the end user — the customer could be someone from a different team inside your own company, or someone from another organization entirely.
2. The team listens to and collects everything the customer says they want. At this stage, the team is only gathering information, not judging or filtering it yet.
3. The team then analyzes what was gathered. This means checking the requirements carefully to make sure they are clear, they do not contradict each other, and nothing important is missing.
4. Only after gathering and analyzing is complete does the team move on to actually planning how to design and develop the software.
5. If this order is skipped — for example, if developers start designing before requirements are fully understood — mismatches happen, and the cost of fixing them later goes up significantly.

### Real-World Example
Think about **Netflix**. Before Netflix builds a new feature, like a "Skip Intro" button, its team has to first identify what users actually want — do users want to skip just the intro song, or also skip the recap of the previous episode? If Netflix's developers guess wrong and only let users skip the intro song, but users were frustrated by the recap too, Netflix would have to rebuild the feature, wasting engineering time and money. This is an example of requirements identification because Netflix must clearly find out and document exactly what "skip" should mean before building the button, instead of guessing and fixing it later.

### Common Mistake
You might think that requirements gathering is just a quick chat with the customer where you ask "what do you want?" and write down the answer. In reality, this is wrong, because customers themselves often do not fully know what they want until they see the wrong thing built. The real skill in requirements identification is asking the right follow-up questions and digging deeper into vague answers, not just collecting the first thing someone says.

### Exam Tip

Be ready to explain this in your own words with the mismatch-leads-to-rework-leads-to-higher-cost chain of reasoning. This is very likely to appear as a short or long answer question, not just an MCQ.

### Remember This
No requirements, no direction — skipping this step always costs more time and money later, not less.

## Concept 2: Stakeholders — Primary, Secondary, and Tertiary Users

### Simple Definition
A **stakeholder** is any person who has an interest in, is affected by, or is involved with a software system in some way. Stakeholders are grouped into three types: primary, secondary, and tertiary users, based on how closely they interact with the system.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because early software teams often made the mistake of thinking about only one type of user — usually whoever directly clicks the buttons in the software. But real software systems affect many more people than just the person typing on the keyboard. The idea of categorizing stakeholders into primary, secondary, and tertiary users was created so that teams do not accidentally forget important people who are impacted by the software, even if those people never open the app at all.

### Why It Matters to You
If you only think about primary users (the people directly using the system), you will completely miss requirements coming from secondary and tertiary users, and your software will fail to support important real-world needs. For example, if you forget that banks (a tertiary user) need certain payment rules followed, your software might break actual financial regulations, which can cause serious legal and business problems, not just a bad user experience.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about a school cafeteria. The students who line up and buy food every day are like **primary users** — they directly use the cafeteria. The school's accountant, who checks the cafeteria's sales reports once a week without ever standing in line, is like a **secondary user** — they use the system's output indirectly, through someone else (the cafeteria staff). Now think about the food delivery truck driver who supplies ingredients to the cafeteria — they never eat there or check the reports, but if the cafeteria changes its menu or opening hours, it directly affects the driver's delivery schedule. This driver is like a **tertiary user** — they do not use the system at all, but they are affected by it. This maps directly to software: primary users use the system directly, secondary users use it through an intermediary, and tertiary users are affected by it without ever touching it.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. First, identify **primary users** — these are the people who frequently and directly interact with the system. In the Amazon Seller Portal example, this includes independent sellers, the sales teams of consumer companies (like those selling mobiles or computers), and independent authors or publishers selling books.
2. Next, identify **secondary users** — these are people who do not use the system directly, but use it through an intermediary. In the same example, sales managers are secondary users because they do not list products themselves, but they periodically check sales numbers, profits, and margins that come from the primary users' activity.
3. Then, identify **tertiary users** — these are people who do not use the software at all, but are still affected by it, or they influence whether the software gets purchased or built a certain way. In the Amazon example, this includes logistics and shipping companies (who deliver the products), banks (who handle the transfer of money to sellers), and even the buyers themselves, since what they purchase depends on what sellers list.
3. Repeat this identification process for every new software project, since the categories of primary, secondary, and tertiary users will be different depending on the system.
4. Make sure requirements are gathered from all three types of users, not just the primary ones, because each group can reveal requirements the others would never mention.

### Real-World Example
Think about **Uber**. The riders and drivers who open the app every day to book or accept rides are **primary users**, since they directly interact with the system. Uber's city operations managers, who do not drive or book rides themselves but check weekly reports on driver earnings and ride demand in their city, are **secondary users**, since they use the system's information through the app's data rather than by riding in cars. Traffic authorities and local governments, who never use the Uber app at all but are affected by Uber's traffic patterns and must approve Uber's operations in their city, are **tertiary users**. This is an example of the primary, secondary, and tertiary user concept because it shows three completely different groups of people connected to the same software system, each with a different level of direct interaction.

### Common Mistake
You might think that only the people who actually click buttons in the app — the primary users — matter when gathering requirements. This is a common and costly mistake. If you ignore secondary users like managers who need specific reports, or tertiary users like banks with strict transaction rules, your software might work fine for the primary users but fail completely when it tries to integrate with the rest of the real world.

### Exam Tip
This is one of the highest-yield topics in this lesson. Expect the exam to directly ask you to define primary, secondary, and tertiary users, and to give examples of each — often using the exact Amazon Seller Portal scenario from the lesson, or a new scenario where you have to classify users yourself. Practice classifying users for a system you have never seen before, since exams often test application, not just memorization.

### Remember This
Primary users click it, secondary users check it through someone else, and tertiary users never touch it but still feel its effects.

## Concept 3: Methods of Requirements Gathering

### Simple Definition
**Methods of requirements gathering** are the different techniques a team can use to actually collect information about what stakeholders want from a software system. These methods go beyond just randomly asking a few users a quick question.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because simply guessing at requirements, or asking only one or two random users, often produces incomplete or biased information. Different stakeholders have different needs and different ways of expressing them, so teams realized they needed a structured toolbox of methods — like interviews and focus groups — to make sure they capture a complete and accurate picture of what is really needed.

### Why It Matters to You
If you rely on only one method, like a single rushed conversation, you risk missing important requirements that only show up through deeper investigation, like reading existing documentation or watching how users actually behave. Using a variety of methods means you catch more requirements, from more types of stakeholders, which means fewer surprises and less expensive rework later in the project.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Imagine you are planning a surprise birthday party and you want to know what kind of cake the birthday person likes. You could just ask them directly (this is like an interview). You could also ask their close friends in a group chat to brainstorm ideas together (this is like a focus group). You could look at old photos of their past birthday parties to see what cakes they have liked before (this is like studying existing documentation). Or you could simply watch what desserts they choose to eat when given options, without asking them at all (this is like observation). Using more than one of these methods together gives you a much more complete and accurate idea of the perfect cake than relying on just one method alone, and this maps directly onto how software teams gather requirements using multiple methods together.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Conduct systematic interviews with users, where the team asks structured questions directly to stakeholders to understand their needs.
2. Study existing documentation relevant to the software system, such as old manuals, reports, or records, which can reveal requirements that people forget to mention out loud.
3. Organize focus groups, which bring together representatives from different categories of users — for example, a mix of primary, secondary, and tertiary users — so the team can hear different perspectives in the same session.
4. Observe user behavior directly in real contexts, watching how people actually use a current system or perform their tasks, since people sometimes do things differently than what they describe in words.
5. Combine multiple methods together rather than relying on just one, since each method tends to reveal different kinds of requirements that the others might miss.

### Real-World Example
Think about how **Spotify** might gather requirements for a new "collaborative playlist" feature. Spotify's team could interview a few users who already create playlists with friends to understand their pain points directly. They could also organize a focus group with casual listeners, music label representatives, and playlist-sharing power users together in one session to hear different views. They might study existing support tickets and forum complaints (documentation) about playlist sharing problems. Finally, they could observe, through app analytics, how users currently try to work around the lack of this feature, such as sharing playlist links manually. This is an example of methods of requirements gathering because Spotify is combining interviews, focus groups, documentation study, and observation to build a complete picture of what users need.

### Common Mistake
You might think that one quick interview with a single user is enough to gather all the requirements needed. This is incorrect, because a single interview only captures one person's perspective, and different methods often reveal completely different requirements. The exam and real software projects both expect you to know that a combination of methods produces far more reliable and complete results than relying on just one.

### Exam Tip
Expect the exam to ask you to simply list and briefly describe the methods of requirements gathering — interviews, studying documentation, focus groups, and observation. This is often tested as a short, direct recall question, so make sure you can name all four methods without hesitation.

### Remember This
Ask them, read about them, group them together, and watch them — four ways to truly understand what users need.

## Concept 4: Requirements Analysis and Common Issues

### Simple Definition
**Requirements analysis** is the step where a team carefully examines the requirements they have already gathered, in order to clearly understand exactly what the customer needs and to find and fix any problems hiding inside those requirements.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because requirements are gathered from many different stakeholders and from many different sources, like interviews, documents, and observations. When you combine information from so many different places, problems naturally creep in — some requirements may be unclear, some may contradict each other, and some may be missing important details. Requirements analysis was created as a dedicated step to catch these problems before development begins, instead of discovering them halfway through building the software.

### Why It Matters to You
If you skip requirements analysis and jump straight from gathering to building, you will carry every unclear, conflicting, or incomplete requirement directly into your code. This means developers will make their own guesses to fill in the gaps, and those guesses will very likely be wrong, leading to the same expensive mismatch and rework problem you learned about in Concept 1.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Imagine you are organizing a group road trip, and everyone in the group chat sends you their travel wishes. One friend says they want to "stop somewhere for food," but does not say where or what kind of food (this is **ambiguous**, like an unclear requirement). Another friend says they want to leave at 6 AM, while a different friend says they want to leave at 9 AM, and both insist on their timing (this is **inconsistent**, like a conflicting requirement). A third friend says they want to "visit the mountains," but never tells you which specific mountain or how long they want to stay there (this is **incomplete**, like a requirement missing key details). Before you actually plan the trip, you would need to go back to each friend and clarify these issues — and this exact double-checking process is what requirements analysis does for software requirements.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Take all the requirements that were gathered from different stakeholders and sources, like interviews, documents, and focus groups.
2. Check each requirement for **ambiguity**, meaning check whether the wording is unclear and could be interpreted in more than one way. For example, the requirement that independent sellers need to "manage their inventory" is ambiguous, because "manage" could mean adding a product, modifying its price, deleting it, or all of these things together, and different people could interpret it differently.
3. Check the requirements for **inconsistency**, meaning check whether two or more requirements contradict each other. For example, independent sellers may want their payments credited every week, while the banking system used by the platform may only allow payments to be credited bi-weekly, creating a direct conflict that developers must resolve before building anything.
4. Check the requirements for **incompleteness**, meaning check whether important details have been left out simply because nobody — neither the customer nor the developer — thought to mention them. For example, a seller asking to "track orders" has left out whether this means tracking delivery status, tracking the order while it is in transit, or tracking which orders have been returned.
5. Once ambiguities, inconsistencies, and incompleteness have been identified, resolve them by going back to stakeholders for clarification, or by clearly documenting constraints (for example, telling sellers that payments can only be processed bi-weekly due to banking limitations).
6. Only once the requirements are clear, consistent, and complete should the team move forward into designing the system.

### Real-World Example
Think about an **online banking** app. Suppose customer feedback says the app should let users "manage beneficiaries." This is ambiguous, because it is unclear if "manage" means only adding new beneficiaries, or also editing and deleting existing ones. Suppose, separately, the security team requires beneficiary additions to be verified within 24 hours, while the customer support team insists new beneficiaries must be usable immediately — this is inconsistent, since the two requirements directly conflict. Suppose a customer also asks for "transaction history," without specifying whether this should include only successful transactions, or also failed and pending ones — this is incomplete. This is an example of requirements analysis because the bank's software team must catch and resolve all three of these problems — ambiguity, inconsistency, and incompleteness — before any developer starts coding the beneficiary or transaction history features.

### Common Mistake
You might think that ambiguity, inconsistency, and incompleteness are basically the same kind of problem, just described with different words. This is incorrect. Ambiguity is about a requirement being unclear or open to multiple meanings. Inconsistency is about two or more requirements directly contradicting each other. Incompleteness is about a requirement missing important details that were never mentioned at all. The exam expects you to clearly tell these three apart, often using examples, so do not blur them together in your answers.

### Exam Tip
This is almost certainly going to be tested with example-based questions, where you are given a requirement and asked to identify whether it shows ambiguity, inconsistency, or incompleteness, or asked to create your own example of each. Make sure you can confidently explain all three using the exact examples from this lesson — manage inventory (ambiguity), weekly versus bi-weekly payments (inconsistency), and track orders (incompleteness) — since these are the lesson's official examples and are highly likely to reappear in some form.

### Remember This
Ambiguity is unclear wording, inconsistency is a direct conflict, and incompleteness is a missing detail — three different problems, three different fixes.

## Key Takeaways

- Skipping requirements gathering and analysis leads to a mismatch between what users expect and what developers build, which increases cost, time, and the number of development iterations needed.
- The two goals of requirement identification are making sure developers understand what customers want, and making sure customers agree that the developers have understood them correctly.
- Stakeholders are grouped into primary users (direct, frequent use), secondary users (indirect use through an intermediary), and tertiary users (no direct use, but affected by or influencing the software).
- In the Amazon Seller Portal example, primary users are sellers, secondary users are sales managers, and tertiary users include logistics companies, banks, and buyers.
- Requirements gathering methods include systematic interviews, studying existing documentation, organizing focus groups, and observing real user behavior, and these methods are most effective when combined.
- Requirement gathering happens first, followed by requirement analysis, and only after both steps are complete should the team move on to system design and development.
- Requirements analysis looks for three main types of problems: ambiguity (unclear wording), inconsistency (contradicting requirements), and incompleteness (missing details).
- Resolving these problems before development begins puts the project team in a much stronger position to design and build the system confidently.

## Exam Essentials

### Important Definitions
- **Requirements Identification** — the process of understanding and documenting what a software system must accomplish before development begins.
- **Stakeholder** — any person who has an interest in, is affected by, or is involved with a software system in some way.
- **Primary Users** — stakeholders who directly and frequently use the software system.
- **Secondary Users** — stakeholders who use the software system indirectly, through an intermediary.
- **Tertiary Users** — stakeholders who do not use the software at all, but are affected by it or influence its purchase or design.
- **Requirements Gathering** — the systematic process of collecting information about what stakeholders need from a software system, using methods such as interviews, documentation study, focus groups, and observation.
- **Requirements Analysis** — the process of examining gathered requirements to form a clear understanding of customer needs and to identify and resolve problems within them.
- **Ambiguity** — a requirement that is unclear and can be interpreted in more than one way.
- **Inconsistency** — two or more requirements that directly contradict each other.
- **Incompleteness** — a requirement that is missing important details that were never specified.

### Potential MCQs

**1. In the Amazon Seller Portal example, who is classified as a secondary user?**
A) Independent sellers
B) Sales managers ✔
C) Logistics companies
D) Buyers

Sales managers are correct because they do not list products directly but check sales data through the sales team, which makes them a secondary user. Independent sellers are wrong because they directly use the system, making them primary users. Logistics companies and buyers are wrong because they do not use the system at all, making them tertiary users.

**2. Which of the following best describes an "ambiguous" requirement?**
A) A requirement that conflicts with another requirement
B) A requirement that is missing key details
C) A requirement that can be interpreted in more than one way ✔
D) A requirement that was never gathered at all

A requirement that can be interpreted in more than one way is correct, as shown by the "manage inventory" example, which could mean adding, editing, or deleting products. Conflicting requirements describe inconsistency, not ambiguity. Missing key details describes incompleteness, not ambiguity. A requirement that was never gathered is not a type of analysis problem at all, since analysis only applies to requirements that have already been collected.

**3. Which step comes immediately after requirements gathering, and before system design?**
A) Coding the software
B) Requirements analysis ✔
C) Testing the software
D) Deploying the software

Requirements analysis is correct because the lesson clearly states the sequence as gathering, then analysis, then design and development. Coding happens only after design, not immediately after gathering. Testing and deploying both happen much later in the development process, long after requirements work is finished.

**4. Why are banks considered tertiary users of the Amazon Seller Portal?**
A) They directly list products for sale
B) They check sales performance reports
C) They are affected by the system through handling money transfers, without using the portal itself ✔
D) They are the main customers of the portal

Banks are correct because they never use the seller portal directly, but they are involved in transferring money to sellers, which makes them affected by the system without using it. Directly listing products describes primary users, not banks. Checking sales reports describes secondary users, like sales managers, not banks. Banks are not the main customers of the portal, since sellers and buyers play that role instead.

**5. The conflict between sellers wanting weekly payments and banks allowing only bi-weekly payments is an example of which requirements analysis issue?**
A) Ambiguity
B) Inconsistency ✔
C) Incompleteness
D) Stakeholder mismatch

Inconsistency is correct because this situation involves two requirements that directly contradict each other. Ambiguity is wrong because both requirements are actually clearly stated, just conflicting, not unclear. Incompleteness is wrong because no details are missing here — the problem is that the existing details clash. "Stakeholder mismatch" is not a term used in this lesson at all.

## Quick Memory Tricks

- **Requirements Identification:** Think "measure twice, cut once" — spending time identifying requirements now is like measuring carefully before cutting fabric, so you do not waste material by cutting wrong and starting over.
- **Primary, Secondary, Tertiary Users:** Picture three rings around a target — primary users are the bullseye who interact directly, secondary users are the next ring out who connect through someone else, and tertiary users are the outer ring who get hit by ripples without ever touching the target.
- **Methods of Gathering:** Remember "I.D.F.O." — Interview, Documentation, Focus groups, Observation — like a detective using four different tools to solve the same case.
- **Ambiguity, Inconsistency, Incompleteness:** Think of three broken promises — ambiguity is a promise nobody understood the same way, inconsistency is two promises that cannot both come true, and incompleteness is a promise that left out the most important part.

---

## One-Line Lesson Summary

Before building any software, teams must carefully gather requirements from primary, secondary, and tertiary stakeholders using methods like interviews and observation, and then analyze those requirements to fix ambiguity, inconsistency, and incompleteness, because skipping these steps leads to costly mismatches between what users expect and what gets built.
