# Week 2 — Lesson 4: Software Requirement Specification (SRS)

## Lesson Overview

Welcome back! You now know how to gather requirements and sort them into functional and non-functional types. Here is the simple version of what comes next: once you have a big pile of sorted requirements, you cannot just hand a messy list to your development team — you need to organize everything into one clear, well-structured document. This lesson introduces that document, called the **Software Requirements Specification**, or **SRS** for short. Think of it like the official blueprint for a house. Before construction workers start building, an architect creates one detailed document showing every room, every door, every electrical socket, and every measurement, so nobody has to guess what to build. The SRS does exactly this for software, and in this lesson, you will learn exactly what goes inside it, why it matters so much, and what its one major drawback is.

---

## Concept 1: Software Requirements Specification (SRS) — Definition and Origin

### Simple Definition
A **Software Requirements Specification (SRS)** is a structured document that organizes all of a software system's user requirements, describing the intended purpose and environment for the software being built. It is the formal, written-down version of everything gathered during requirements gathering and analysis.

### Why It Exists
The SRS exists because simply collecting requirements through interviews, questionnaires, and observation is not enough — all of that scattered information needs to be organized into one official document that everyone can refer to. In the plan and document software process model, a person called a **system analyst** gathers all this information, in consultation with other members of the software team, and then organizes it into the SRS. Many SRS documents follow a recognized standard called **IEEE 830**, which gives teams a common, well-tested format to follow instead of inventing their own structure from scratch every time.

### Why It Matters to You
If you skip creating a proper SRS, all of your gathered requirements remain scattered across interview notes, questionnaire results, and meeting summaries, with no single place anyone can check to confirm exactly what the software must do. This matters because developers, customers, and managers all need to look at the same organized document to stay aligned; without it, miscommunication and mismatched expectations creep back in, even after all the hard work of gathering requirements properly.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about planning a wedding. You might have spoken to the caterer, the photographer, the venue manager, and the decorator separately, and each conversation gave you a pile of notes and preferences. But at some point, you need one single, organized wedding plan document that lists everything in order: the venue details, the guest list, the food menu, the photography schedule, and the decoration plan, all in one place that anyone involved can check. The SRS works exactly the same way for software — it takes everything learned from separate conversations with different stakeholders and organizes it into one clear, structured document that the whole team can rely on.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. A system analyst gathers and analyzes requirements from various stakeholders, using techniques like interviews, questionnaires, and documentation review, as covered in earlier lessons.
2. The system analyst then organizes all of this gathered information into the structured SRS document, following a standard format such as IEEE 830.
3. The SRS is shared with the customers, who read through it carefully to check that it matches what they actually want.
4. After several rounds of feedback and iteration, the SRS document is finally accepted by both the customers and the development team.
5. Once accepted, the SRS becomes the reference document that guides the rest of the software development process, including design, development, and testing.

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal**. Before development begins, the system analyst would gather everything learned from interviewing sellers, observing sales managers, and reviewing bank documentation, and would organize it all into one official SRS document. This document would clearly state that sellers can manage their catalog, that products must appear within five seconds of being added, and that the system needs to be reliable and robust. This is an example of a Software Requirements Specification because it takes previously scattered, separately-gathered requirements and organizes them into one single, structured reference document.

### Common Mistake
You might think the SRS is something written once, at the very start, and then left untouched for the rest of the project. This is a common misconception. While the SRS is mainly created early in the plan and document process, it represents an agreement that should remain accurate, and parts of it may need updates if the project's understanding of requirements changes, rather than being treated as a permanently frozen, one-time document.

### Remember This
The SRS is the official blueprint of the software — it turns scattered conversations into one document everyone can build from.

## Concept 2: Structure of an SRS Document

### Simple Definition
The **structure of an SRS document** refers to its standard table of contents, made up of numbered sections and subsections that organize the software's requirements in a predictable, logical order.

### Why It Exists
This standard structure exists so that anyone familiar with SRS documents can quickly find the information they need, without having to read the entire document from start to finish. If every team invented its own random structure, developers moving between projects would have to relearn a new document layout every single time, wasting time and increasing the chance of missing important details.

### Why It Matters to You
If an SRS is poorly organized, important requirements can get lost or overlooked simply because nobody knew where to look for them. A clear, standard structure means that a new developer joining the project, or a manager reviewing the document months later, can quickly locate exactly the section they need, such as interface requirements or non-functional requirements, without confusion.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about a school textbook. Every textbook has a table of contents, an introduction chapter explaining what the subject covers, then detailed chapters covering specific topics in a logical order, like chapter 1 on basics, then later chapters on advanced material. You do not need to read the whole book to find what you need; you can jump straight to the right chapter because the structure is predictable. The SRS works the same way: it has a predictable structure, so anyone can jump straight to the section that matters to them, whether that is the broad overview or the very detailed requirements.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. The SRS begins with a **table of contents**, listing all the sections and subsections so readers can quickly navigate to what they need.
2. **Section 1 — Introduction** describes the broad outline of the software system, including its purpose, scope, definitions, acronyms, abbreviations, references, and a general overview of what the software is for.
3. **Section 2 — Overall Description** explains the bigger picture, including the product's perspective (how it fits into a larger ecosystem of other systems), a summary of product functions, user characteristics, and the project's constraints, assumptions, and dependencies.
4. **Section 3 — Specific Requirements** is the most detailed section, containing the precise functional and non-functional requirements of the system, broken down further into subsections.
5. Within Section 3, subsection 3.1 covers **external interface requirements**, and subsection 3.2 covers **system features**, both of which you will learn about in the next two concepts.
6. Subsections 3.3 through 3.6 cover the remaining non-functional requirements, such as **performance requirements** (speed and resource standards, as covered in the previous lesson), **design constraints** (specific limitations the design must follow, such as required technologies or platforms), and **software system attributes** (other overall quality attributes of the system, such as reliability and security, also covered in the previous lesson).
7. Remember that this structure is a guideline, not a rigid rule — depending on the organization and context, modifications to the structure and content are completely possible.

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal** SRS. Section 1 would explain that the purpose of the system is to let independent sellers, sales teams, and authors list and sell products through Amazon. Section 2 would describe how the seller portal fits into the larger Amazon ecosystem, alongside the buying portal and banking systems. Section 3 would then list specific requirements, such as the external interface with the Amazon buying portal database, system features like catalog management and order tracking, and non-functional requirements like reliability and five-second response times. This is an example of SRS structure because it shows information flowing from a broad overview down to highly specific, organized requirement details.

### Common Mistake
You might think the SRS structure is fixed and must be followed exactly the same way for every single project, since it is described as a "standard." This is incorrect. The lesson explicitly states that this structure is a guideline, and modifications to it are possible depending on the organization and context, as long as the document still organizes requirements clearly.

### Exam Tip

Be ready to also name the two key subsections of Section 3 covered in detail in this lesson: external interface requirements (3.1) and system features (3.2).

### Remember This
Section 1 tells you the big picture, Section 2 tells you how it fits in, and Section 3 tells you the exact, detailed requirements.

## Concept 3: External Interface Requirements

### Simple Definition
**External interface requirements** are the specific details, found in Section 3.1 of the SRS, that describe exactly how the software will interact with users, hardware, other software, and communication systems outside of itself.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because software rarely works completely on its own — it almost always needs to connect to something else, whether that is a human user looking at a screen, a piece of hardware like a card reader, another software system like a database, or a communication channel like email. Without clearly specifying these connections in advance, a development team might build a feature that simply cannot properly connect to the other systems it depends on.

### Why It Matters to You
If external interface requirements are not clearly specified, your software might be built correctly on its own, but fail completely when it tries to actually talk to a piece of hardware, another software system, or a communication service it depends on. For example, if nobody specifies exactly how the Amazon Seller Portal needs to interface with the Amazon buying portal's database, sellers' products might never actually appear for buyers to purchase, even though the seller-side features work perfectly.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about a power plug. For your phone charger to actually work, it needs to interface correctly with several different things: the wall socket (hardware interface), the phone's charging port (another hardware interface), the electrical grid supplying power (a kind of communication interface), and you, the human, who needs a clearly labeled charging light to know it is working (a user interface). If any one of these connections is not designed correctly, your phone simply will not charge, even if the charger itself is perfectly built. External interface requirements work the same way for software — they ensure every single connection point to the outside world is clearly specified and actually works.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Specify the **user interface (UI)** requirements, including sample screen images, GUI standards, and screen layout, since these describe exactly what the user will see and interact with.
2. Specify the **hardware interfaces**, describing the supported device types and the nature of the data and control interactions between the software and the hardware. For example, in an ATM system, you must specify the interface between the ATM hardware, the ATM card, and the ATM software.
3. Specify the **software interfaces**, describing the connections between this software and other software components, such as databases, operating systems, and other tools and libraries. For example, the Amazon Seller Portal must interface with the Amazon buying portal database, since products listed by sellers must become visible and purchasable there.
4. Specify the **communication interfaces**, describing any communication the software requires with outside services. For example, the seller portal needs to interface with an email or SMS server to send order notifications to sellers and buyers.
5. Document each of these four interface types clearly in Section 3.1 of the SRS, since missing even one of them can cause real integration failures once development begins.

### Real-World Example
Think about an **online banking** system using a payment feature similar to UPI-style transfers. The user interface is the banking app screen where the customer enters a payment amount. The software interface is the connection to the partner bank's API, which actually processes the transfer of money. The communication interface is the internet connection required to send the request to the bank's servers and receive a confirmation back. This is an example of external interface requirements because it shows three different connection points — user, software, and communication — that must all be specified for a single payment feature to work correctly.

### Common Mistake
You might think external interface requirements only refer to what the user sees on screen, since "interface" often makes people think of just the visual design. This is incomplete. External interface requirements also cover hardware connections, software-to-software connections like databases, and communication connections like email or SMS servers — all four types must be specified, not just the user-facing screen.

### Exam Tip

Exams may give you a new scenario and ask you to identify which interface type is being described.

### Remember This
Software almost never works alone — user, hardware, software, and communication interfaces are the four doors it needs to connect through.

## Concept 4: System Features

### Simple Definition
**System features**, found in Section 3.2 of the SRS, are the high-level functions of the software, each one grouped together with its own set of detailed functional requirements.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because a long, unorganized list of individual functional requirements can quickly become overwhelming and hard to navigate. By grouping related functional requirements under a clear, high-level feature name, the SRS becomes much easier to read and understand, since readers can first see the big picture (the feature) before diving into its specific details (the functional requirements underneath it).

### Why It Matters to You
If functional requirements are not grouped into clear system features, a developer or customer reviewing the SRS would have to read through a giant, unorganized list of small details with no clear sense of which details belong together. Organizing requirements under system features helps everyone quickly understand the major capabilities of the software at a glance, before getting into the finer details of each one.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about a restaurant menu. Instead of listing every single ingredient for every dish in one giant unorganized list, a good menu groups dishes under clear high-level categories, like "Starters," "Main Courses," and "Desserts." Within each category, you then see the specific details of each dish. This is exactly how system features work in an SRS — broad categories, like "catalog management" or "order tracking," group together the specific functional requirements that belong under each one, making the whole document much easier to navigate.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Identify the major, high-level functions the software needs to support, known as system features. For example, the Amazon Seller Portal includes system features like managing the catalog, tracking inventory, tracking orders, and tracking payments.
2. For each system feature, write a brief **introduction or purpose**, explaining what that feature is meant to accomplish at a high level.
3. Describe the **stimulus and response sequence** for the feature, meaning the sequence of user actions (stimulus) and the system's corresponding reactions (response) involved in using that feature.
4. List the **associated functional requirements** underneath each system feature, detailing the specific actions and outputs that make up that feature, exactly as you learned about functional requirements in the previous lesson.
5. Repeat this process for every major feature identified for the system, ensuring that every functional requirement is grouped under a clear, high-level feature name.

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal** again. "Catalog Management" is a system feature, grouping together the functional requirements that let a seller add, edit, or delete products. "Order Tracking" is another system feature, grouping together the functional requirements related to tracking the status of customer orders. "Payment Tracking" similarly groups together the requirements related to monitoring incoming payments. This is an example of system features because each one acts as a high-level container, organizing several related, detailed functional requirements underneath one clear name.

### Common Mistake
You might think system features and functional requirements are exactly the same thing, just with different names. They are related but not identical: a system feature is the high-level grouping or category, like "catalog management," while functional requirements are the specific, detailed actions and outputs that exist underneath that feature, like "seller can add a product" or "seller can delete a product."

### Exam Tip
Be ready to explain the three components that make up a system feature's description in the SRS: its introduction or purpose, its stimulus and response sequence, and its associated functional requirements. Exams may also ask you to take a real-world feature, like catalog management, and identify which functional requirements would logically belong underneath it.

### Remember This
A system feature is the menu category; functional requirements are the individual dishes listed underneath it.

## Concept 5: Advantages of Maintaining an SRS

### Simple Definition
The **advantages of maintaining an SRS** are the practical benefits a software team gains by keeping a well-structured, accurate Software Requirements Specification throughout a project.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because creating an SRS takes real time and effort, so it is important to understand exactly why this investment is worthwhile. The lesson identifies four specific advantages that justify the effort of creating and maintaining this document, rather than skipping it or treating requirements informally.

### Why It Matters to You
If you do not understand the real benefits of an SRS, you might be tempted to skip this step entirely to save time, especially under project pressure. This would be a costly mistake, because the SRS directly helps prevent miscommunication, reduces expensive rework, supports accurate cost and schedule estimation, and makes future improvements to the software much easier to plan.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about signing a rental agreement before moving into an apartment. The agreement clearly states what you are getting (the apartment, its condition, its included furniture) and what the landlord expects from you (rent amount, rules about pets, move-out notice). Because this is written down clearly in advance, there is much less chance of a dispute later, since both sides already agreed on the exact terms. The SRS works the same way between customers and developers — it clearly documents what is expected, in advance, which prevents costly disputes and confusion later in the project.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. **Forms an agreement between customers and developers** — after the SRS is prepared, it is shared with customers, who review it and, after several iterations, finally accept it, giving both sides a shared, agreed-upon understanding of what will be built.
2. **Reduces future reworks** — because creating an SRS forces stakeholders to think rigorously about all requirements before design and development even begin, fewer changes are needed later, which saves both time and money.
3. **Provides a basis for estimating costs and schedules** — an SRS helps estimate the overall size of the software, since size is essentially a function of all the requirements listed, and this size estimate then feeds into estimating effort, cost, and project schedules.
4. **Facilitates future extensions** — an SRS serves as a clear reference document for planning future enhancements or extensions to the software, since anyone working on the system later can read the SRS to understand exactly how it was originally designed to work.

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal** project. Once the SRS is accepted by both Amazon's internal teams and any external development partners, everyone agrees on exactly what features, like catalog management and order tracking, will be delivered. Because all requirements were carefully thought through in advance, the development team avoids major surprises and expensive design changes midway through the project. The project manager can also use the SRS's listed requirements to estimate how long development will take and how much it will cost. This is an example of the advantages of maintaining an SRS because it shows agreement, reduced rework, and cost estimation all happening because one clear document was created and accepted early on.

### Common Mistake
You might think the main benefit of an SRS is simply "having paperwork" to show that requirements were discussed. This undersells its real value. The SRS provides four distinct, concrete benefits — agreement, reduced rework, cost and schedule estimation, and easier future extensions — and each one directly saves the project time, money, or confusion, rather than just being a formality.

### Exam Tip
Be ready to list and briefly explain all four advantages of maintaining an SRS: forming agreement, reducing rework, estimating costs and schedules, and facilitating future extensions. Exam questions often ask you to explain why an SRS is valuable, so make sure you can name more than just one advantage.

### Remember This
A good SRS pays for itself by preventing disagreements, preventing rework, enabling accurate estimates, and making future upgrades easier.

## Concept 6: Limitations of SRS and the Move Toward Agile

### Simple Definition
The **limitations of SRS** refer to the practical drawbacks of creating and maintaining such a detailed document, the most significant of which is the large amount of documentation effort required.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because, despite all of its advantages, the SRS approach is not perfect, and it is important to understand its weaknesses honestly. The plan and document model, which relies heavily on the SRS, works well when requirements are fixed and unlikely to change, but real software projects do not always have stable, unchanging requirements.

### Why It Matters to You
If you treat the SRS approach as the only correct way to manage requirements in every situation, you risk wasting enormous amounts of time maintaining detailed documentation for a project where requirements are constantly changing, causing the SRS to become outdated almost as quickly as it is written. Recognizing this limitation helps you understand why other approaches, like the agile perspective and behavior-driven design, exist as lighter alternatives.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about writing out a complete, detailed travel itinerary for a relaxed vacation where your plans might change daily based on the weather or your mood. Writing every single detail in advance, hour by hour, would take a huge amount of effort, and you would likely have to rewrite large parts of it anyway once your plans inevitably change. In this situation, a lighter, more flexible plan that adapts as you go would clearly work better than one giant, rigid document. This is exactly the limitation of the SRS — it requires a lot of upfront documentation effort, which works well only when requirements are stable, but becomes wasteful and outdated quickly when requirements keep changing.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Recognize that creating a full SRS requires extensive documentation, covering introduction, overall description, and very specific requirements across multiple subsections.
2. Understand that this heavy documentation effort is worthwhile and works well specifically when the project's requirements are fixed and not expected to change significantly.
3. Recognize the problem that arises when requirements are not fixed: as requirements change, the existing SRS quickly becomes outdated, and maintaining it requires constant rework of the document itself.
4. Understand that this specific drawback is one of the key reasons the agile perspective exists, particularly approaches like behavior-driven design, which aim to keep documentation lighter and more adaptable to change.
5. Remember the important exam distinction: even agile approaches still require some documentation — they simply make that documentation lighter and more flexible, rather than eliminating it entirely.

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal** once again. If Amazon's core seller features, like catalog management and order tracking, are well understood and unlikely to change much, writing a full, detailed SRS up front works well and saves time overall. However, if Amazon decides to rapidly experiment with new seller tools and frequently changes what features it wants, constantly rewriting a heavy SRS to keep up with every change would become a huge burden, making a lighter, more flexible agile approach more practical instead. This is an example of the limitation of SRS because it shows the exact same project benefiting from SRS in one scenario, but struggling with it in another, depending on how stable the requirements are.

### Common Mistake
You might think that agile approaches completely eliminate the need for any documentation at all, since they are positioned as the lighter alternative to a heavy SRS. This is a common and important exam trap. Documentation is always needed in some form — agile approaches simply make that documentation lighter and more adaptable, they do not remove the need for it entirely.

### Exam Tip
Remember this exact exam alert: any answer choice claiming that an approach "eliminates the need for documentation" is almost always false. Be ready to explain that the SRS's main drawback is the large amount of documentation required, that this works well only when requirements are fixed, and that agile and behavior-driven design exist specifically to address this drawback with lighter, not zero, documentation.

### Remember This
Heavy documentation works great when requirements stand still — but the moment requirements start moving, you need a lighter approach, not zero documentation.

## Key Takeaways

- A Software Requirements Specification (SRS) is a structured document, often following the IEEE 830 standard, that organizes all of a software system's requirements after gathering and analysis.
- The SRS is typically created by a system analyst in the plan and document software process model, and is reviewed and accepted by customers after several iterations.
- The SRS follows a standard structure: Section 1 (Introduction), Section 2 (Overall Description), and Section 3 (Specific Requirements), though this structure is a flexible guideline, not a rigid rule.
- Section 3.1, external interface requirements, covers four types of interfaces: user, hardware, software, and communication, each connecting the software to something outside itself.
- Section 3.2, system features, groups detailed functional requirements under high-level feature names, each described by its purpose, stimulus-response sequence, and associated functional requirements.
- Maintaining an SRS provides four key advantages: forming agreement between customers and developers, reducing future reworks, supporting cost and schedule estimation, and facilitating future extensions.
- The main drawback of an SRS is the large amount of documentation it requires, which works well only when requirements are fixed, and becomes a burden when requirements change frequently.
- Agile approaches and behavior-driven design exist to address this drawback by making documentation lighter and more adaptable, but they do not eliminate documentation entirely.

## Exam Essentials

### Important Definitions
- **Software Requirements Specification (SRS)** — a structured document describing the intended purpose, environment, and requirements of a software system.
- **System Analyst** — the person who gathers and analyzes requirements and organizes them into the SRS, in the plan and document model.
- **IEEE 830** — a recognized standard format that many SRS documents follow.
- **External Interface Requirements** — specifications describing how the software interacts with users, hardware, other software, and communication systems.
- **System Features** — high-level functions of the software, each grouped with its own associated functional requirements.
- **Stimulus/Response Sequence** — the sequence of user actions (stimulus) and the system's corresponding reactions (response) for a given system feature.

### Potential MCQs

**1. Who typically gathers and organizes requirements into an SRS document in the plan and document model?**
A) The end customer
B) The system analyst ✔
C) The project manager
D) The software tester

The system analyst is correct because the lesson specifically states that this person gathers requirements in consultation with the software team and organizes them into the SRS. The end customer provides requirements but does not typically organize them into the formal document. The project manager and software tester are not described in this lesson as responsible for creating the SRS.

**2. Which section of the SRS contains the most detailed functional and non-functional requirements?**
A) Section 1 — Introduction
B) Section 2 — Overall Description
C) Section 3 — Specific Requirements ✔
D) The Table of Contents

Section 3 is correct because the lesson explicitly describes it as containing the specific functional and non-functional requirements of the system. Section 1 only provides a broad outline and description. Section 2 covers the bigger picture, like product perspective and constraints, not detailed requirements. The Table of Contents simply lists sections; it does not contain requirement details itself.

**3. In an ATM system, the interface between the ATM card, the ATM hardware, and the ATM software is an example of which type of external interface requirement?**
A) User interface
B) Hardware interface ✔
C) Software interface
D) Communication interface

Hardware interface is correct because the lesson uses this exact ATM example to illustrate the connection between hardware and software. A user interface would describe what the user sees on a screen, not the hardware connection itself. A software interface describes connections between software components like databases, not physical hardware. A communication interface describes things like email or SMS notifications, not a card-and-machine connection.

**4. What is the main purpose of grouping functional requirements under "system features" in an SRS?**
A) To make the document shorter overall
B) To organize related functional requirements under a clear, high-level category ✔
C) To replace the need for non-functional requirements
D) To avoid specifying external interfaces

Organizing related functional requirements under a clear, high-level category is correct, since this is exactly why system features exist, making the document easier to read and navigate. Grouping does not necessarily make the document shorter, since the same level of detail is still included, just organized differently. System features do not replace non-functional requirements, which remain a separate part of Section 3. System features are unrelated to external interface requirements, which are covered separately in Section 3.1.

**5. According to the exam alert in this lesson, which statement about agile approaches is almost always FALSE?**
A) Agile approaches make documentation lighter
B) Agile approaches eliminate the need for documentation entirely ✔
C) Agile approaches help address the heavy documentation drawback of SRS
D) Agile approaches are often more adaptable to changing requirements

The statement that agile approaches eliminate documentation entirely is correct as the false one, since the lesson's exam alert specifically warns that documentation is always needed, and agile just makes it lighter. It is true that agile approaches make documentation lighter, which is the opposite of the false claim. It is also true that agile approaches address the SRS's heavy documentation drawback and are more adaptable to changing requirements, both of which are accurate statements from the lesson.

## Quick Memory Tricks

- **SRS Definition:** Think "wedding plan document" — it takes scattered conversations with different people and turns them into one organized plan everyone can follow.
- **SRS Structure:** Remember "Intro, Overview, Details" for Sections 1, 2, and 3 — starting broad and getting more specific as you go deeper into the document.
- **External Interfaces:** Remember "U-H-S-C" — User, Hardware, Software, Communication — the four doors software connects through to the outside world.
- **System Features:** Picture a restaurant menu — broad categories like "Starters" group together the specific dishes (functional requirements) listed underneath.
- **SRS Drawback:** Remember "heavy document, happy only when things stay still" — SRS works great for fixed requirements, but becomes a burden the moment requirements start changing.

## One-Line Lesson Summary

A Software Requirements Specification organizes all of a system's gathered requirements into one structured document with sections for introduction, overall description, external interfaces, and system features, offering real advantages like agreement and reduced rework, but its heavy documentation requirement works best only when requirements stay fixed, which is why lighter agile approaches also exist.
