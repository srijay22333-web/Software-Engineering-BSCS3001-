# Week 2 — Lesson 5: Behaviour Driven Design (BDD) — User Stories

## Lesson Overview

Welcome back! In the last lesson, you learned that the SRS document works really well when requirements are fixed and stable, but becomes a heavy burden when requirements keep changing. Here is the simple version of what comes next: this lesson introduces a completely different way of handling requirements, built specifically for situations where things keep changing — the agile approach, starting with something called **Behaviour Driven Design (BDD)**. Think of it like the difference between writing one giant, fixed travel itinerary for a two-week holiday versus planning just a few days at a time and adjusting your plans as you go, based on the weather and how you feel. In this lesson, you will learn about BDD, its lightweight replacement for the SRS called **User Stories**, the simple template used to write them, and the **SMART** checklist that makes sure every user story is actually good. This is one of the most exam-important lessons in this module, so let's dive in.

## Concept 1: Limitations of Traditional SRS and the Need for Agile

### Simple Definition
This concept describes the core problem that motivates Behaviour Driven Design: in many real software projects, **customers themselves are unsure of their requirements**, and their understanding of what they need evolves and changes over time, making a fixed SRS document difficult to maintain.

### Why It Exists
This problem exists because requirements gathering assumes that, at some point, you can write down a final, stable list of what the customer wants. But in reality, many customers do not fully know what they want until they actually start seeing a working version of the software. As the previous lesson explained, the SRS approach works well only when requirements are fixed — so a different approach was needed for situations where requirements are expected to keep evolving.

### Why It Matters to You
If you try to force a fixed, heavy SRS document onto a project where the customer's needs are still evolving, you will end up constantly rewriting the document, wasting huge amounts of time keeping it updated, while the actual software development falls behind. Recognizing this problem early helps you choose the right approach — sticking with the SRS for stable projects, or switching to an agile, BDD-based approach for projects where requirements are expected to change.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about asking a friend to plan a surprise party for you. If you tell them every single detail in advance — the exact decorations, the exact guest list, the exact food — and then change your mind about the theme halfway through planning, your friend has to redo a large part of their plan. Now imagine instead that your friend checks in with you every few days, shows you a small piece of the plan, like just the decorations first, and adjusts based on your feedback before moving on to the next piece, like the food. This second approach handles your changing mind much more smoothly, and this is exactly the situation that pushed software teams toward agile and BDD instead of relying only on a fixed SRS.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Recognize that, in many projects, customers are unsure of their exact requirements at the very start, and their understanding evolves as the project progresses.
2. Recognize that a traditional, fixed SRS document struggles to keep up with this kind of constant change, since it was designed to capture a stable, agreed-upon set of requirements.
3. Apply the agile perspective, which involves working closely and interacting frequently with stakeholders to develop and refine requirements continuously, rather than fixing them once at the start.
4. Build a working prototype early, and refine it by adding new features in short cycles called sprints or iterations, which typically run about every two weeks.
5. Begin this agile life cycle with Behaviour Driven Design, which you will learn about in the next concept, as the starting point for continuously refining requirements throughout development.

### Real-World Example
Think about **Spotify**. When Spotify first introduced collaborative playlists, it is unlikely that every single detail, like exactly how many people could edit a playlist or how conflicts between edits would be handled, was perfectly fixed in advance. Instead, Spotify likely built a basic version, gathered feedback from real users, and refined the feature over several updates based on what users actually wanted once they started using it. This is an example of the need for an agile approach because the exact requirements only became clear through real usage and feedback, not through one fixed, upfront document.

### Common Mistake
You might think that needing an agile approach instead of a fixed SRS means that the customer or the team did a poor job of requirements gathering. This is not necessarily true. Even with excellent requirements gathering techniques, some customers genuinely cannot know their exact needs until they see a working version of the software, and this is a normal, expected situation that agile and BDD are specifically designed to handle well.

### Exam Tip
Be ready to explain clearly why traditional SRS struggles in some projects: because customer requirements can be unsure and can evolve over time, making fixed documentation difficult to maintain. This sets up the reasoning for why BDD and user stories exist, so exams often ask you to connect this limitation directly to the motivation for agile approaches.

### Remember This
When the customer's mind keeps changing, a fixed document keeps breaking — that is exactly when agile and BDD step in.

## Concept 2: Behaviour Driven Design (BDD)

### Simple Definition
**Behaviour Driven Design (BDD)** is an agile approach that asks questions about how an application should behave, both before and during development, so that stakeholders are less likely to miscommunicate with each other.

### Why It Exists
BDD exists because, instead of trying to write one final, fixed document like an SRS, teams needed a way to keep requirements flexible and continuously refined, while still writing things down clearly enough to avoid miscommunication. BDD focuses specifically on behaviour — what the application should actually do from the user's point of view — rather than getting caught up in implementation details too early.

### Why It Matters to You
If you skip a behaviour-focused approach and jump straight into discussing how to implement something technically, you risk losing sight of what the user actually needs, and you risk locking in technical decisions before requirements are even fully understood. BDD matters because it keeps the conversation focused on real user behaviour first, which leads to better, more relevant software, and it allows requirements to keep being refined as development continues, rather than being frozen too early.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about training a new employee at a coffee shop. Instead of handing them one giant rulebook on day one covering every possible situation, a good manager asks specific behaviour-focused questions as situations come up, like "what should you do if a customer wants extra foam?" and refines the employee's understanding step by step, through ongoing conversation. This is much more like BDD than handing over one fixed manual — BDD keeps asking and refining questions about behaviour throughout the process, rather than trying to nail everything down perfectly on day one.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Begin asking questions about how the application should behave, focusing on the user's perspective, before development starts.
2. Continue asking and refining these behaviour-focused questions throughout development, not just once at the beginning.
3. Write down the answers to these questions, but keep them as lightweight, easy-to-change requirements, rather than one large, rigid document.
4. Use these continuously refined requirements to guide what gets built next, allowing the requirements to evolve as stakeholders' understanding improves.
5. Represent these requirements using a specific, lightweight format known as **user stories**, which you will learn about in the next concept, instead of the heavier SRS format used in the plan and document model.

### Real-World Example
Think about **Netflix**. Suppose Netflix's team wants to build a "Watch Party" feature where friends can watch a show together remotely. Instead of writing one giant fixed document describing every technical detail in advance, the team would likely ask behaviour-focused questions, like "what should happen if one friend pauses the video — should it pause for everyone?" and refine their understanding through conversation and quick prototypes. This is an example of Behaviour Driven Design because the team is focusing on how the feature should behave from the user's perspective, and continuously refining this understanding, rather than locking in one fixed, detailed specification upfront.

### Common Mistake
You might think BDD means there is no documentation at all, since it avoids the heavy SRS format. This is incorrect. BDD still involves writing things down — it simply uses a much lighter, more flexible format called user stories, instead of a large, fixed SRS document, and these are continuously refined rather than treated as a one-time, final document.

### Exam Tip
Be ready to define BDD precisely: it asks questions about the behaviour of an application before and during development, so that stakeholders are less likely to miscommunicate. Exams may ask you to contrast this directly with the SRS approach from the previous lesson, so be ready to explain that BDD replaces the SRS with continuously refined, lightweight requirements.

### Remember This
BDD keeps asking "how should this behave?" again and again throughout development, instead of answering it once and freezing the answer forever.

## Concept 3: User Stories

### Simple Definition
A **user story** is a short, informal, plain-language description of what a user wants to do within a software product, written in a way that highlights the value or benefit it provides to that user. User stories are the BDD version of requirements, replacing the SRS used in the plan and document model.

### Why It Exists
User stories exist because BDD needed a lightweight, easy-to-write-and-change format for capturing requirements, instead of the heavy, detailed SRS document. User stories originally came from the human-computer interaction (HCI) community, and were traditionally written on small three-by-five-inch index cards, which naturally kept them short and focused, rather than long and detailed like an SRS section.

### Why It Matters to You
If you tried to use a full SRS-style document for every small piece of an agile project, you would lose the flexibility and speed that agile development depends on. User stories matter because they let a team capture just enough information to build a small, valuable piece of functionality quickly, in roughly one sprint (one to two weeks), without the overhead of a long specification document.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about the difference between a full restaurant cookbook and a simple recipe card. A cookbook chapter might describe the entire history, theory, and many variations of a dish in great technical detail. A simple recipe card just tells you what you need and why you are making it — quick, clear, and easy to act on immediately. A user story is like that recipe card: a short, clear, plain-language description that tells you what is needed and why, without the heavy detail of a full specification document like the SRS.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Identify a specific type of user, known as the **role**, who will benefit from the feature. For example, an independent seller using the Amazon Seller Portal.
2. Identify the specific action this user wants to perform, known as the **feature**. For example, the seller wants to view their inventory.
3. Identify the specific value or benefit this action provides to the user, known as the **benefit**. For example, the seller can identify products that are running low in number.
4. Write the user story using the standard **Role–Feature–Benefit template**: "As a [role], I want [feature], so that [benefit]."
5. Keep the user story small enough to be completed within a single sprint, which is typically one to two weeks, since user stories represent the smallest unit of work that can be completed in one sprint.

### Visual Diagram

```
|              USER STORY: ROLE-FEATURE-BENEFIT TEMPLATE           |
+----------------------------------------------------------------+

   ROLE                    FEATURE                  BENEFIT
   +-------------+         +-------------------+    +----------------------+
   | "As an        |       | "I want to view     |   | "So that I can         |
   | independent    | ----> | my inventory"        |--> | identify products       |

```
This diagram shows how a user story breaks down into three connected parts — who, what, and why — using the Role-Feature-Benefit template in plain English.

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal**. The exact user story used in the lesson is: "As an independent seller, I want to view my inventory, so that I can identify products that are running low in number." Another example is: "As an independent seller, I want to view my customers' feedback for each product, so that I can understand issues in my products." This is an example of a user story because each one is short, written in plain language, and clearly identifies who wants it, what they want, and why it benefits them, instead of a long, formal specification.

### Common Mistake
You might think a user story is exactly the same thing as a functional requirement from the SRS, since both describe an action a user wants to perform. They are related but different: a functional requirement in an SRS is typically written in a more formal, detailed, document-style format as part of a larger specification, while a user story is short, informal, plain-language, and explicitly includes the "why" — the benefit or value — which a basic functional requirement statement does not always emphasize.

### Exam Tip
Memorize the exact Role-Feature-Benefit template: "As a [role], I want [feature], so that [benefit]." Exams frequently ask you to write a user story for a given scenario using this exact template, or to identify the role, feature, and benefit within a given user story.

### Remember This
A user story always answers three questions in one short sentence: who wants it, what do they want, and why does it matter to them.

## Concept 4: Benefits of User Stories

### Simple Definition
The **benefits of user stories** are the practical advantages teams gain by using this lightweight format instead of a heavy SRS document, including easier prioritization, better focus on behaviour, and improved conversations between stakeholders.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because it is important to understand exactly why teams choose user stories over a full SRS in agile projects, beyond just saying they are "lighter." The lesson identifies specific, practical advantages that make user stories genuinely useful tools, not just a shortcut.

### Why It Matters to You
If you do not understand why user stories are beneficial, you might underestimate their value and assume they are simply a lazier, less thorough version of proper requirements. Understanding these specific benefits helps you see why agile teams rely on user stories so heavily, and how they actually improve communication and flexibility rather than just reducing paperwork.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about planning a group hiking trip using a quick group chat instead of a formal travel itinerary. In the group chat, you can quickly decide which trail to do first based on everyone's preference (prioritization), focus the conversation on what everyone wants to experience rather than the technical details of how to get there (behaviour, not implementation), and naturally let new ideas come up during the conversation, like "let's also bring a picnic" (facilitating conversation). This relaxed, flexible communication style captures exactly why user stories work so well compared to a long, formal document.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. **Lightweight documentation** — user stories avoid the heavy documentation overhead of an SRS, since they only need a short, plain-language sentence rather than detailed specification sections.
2. **Helps plan and prioritize** — because each user story represents a small, separable piece of work, teams can decide which features to build first. For example, if a client wants the view inventory feature urgently, the development team can simply tell them, "we will deliver the view inventory feature in two weeks."
3. **Focus on behaviour, not implementation** — user stories deliberately avoid specifying how a feature will be built, concentrating only on what the user needs and why, which reduces misunderstandings between stakeholders about technical details that do not concern the customer.
4. **Facilitates conversation between users and developers** — the real value often comes not just from writing the story itself, but from the back-and-forth conversation it sparks. For example, when shown the customer feedback user story, a client might realize they also want to be notified by email about products receiving very low ratings, and this kind of modification is common and easily accommodated in the agile perspective.

```
   +----------------------+        +----------------------+

   +----------------------+        +----------------------+

   +----------------------+        +----------------------+
```

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal** customer feedback user story again. When this story is shown to a real client, the client might say, "I'd also like to be notified by email about products that get one or two star ratings." Because the user story sparked a natural conversation rather than a rigid document review, this new need was captured easily and could be added to the backlog of future user stories. This is an example of the benefits of user stories because it shows exactly how facilitating conversation led to discovering a valuable new requirement that a fixed, one-time document might have missed.

### Common Mistake
You might think that focusing on "behaviour, not implementation" means developers are not allowed to discuss any technical details at all. This is not quite accurate — it simply means the user story itself should describe what the user needs and why, without dictating the specific technical solution, leaving the development team free to decide the best way to implement it.

### Exam Tip
Be ready to list and briefly explain all four benefits: lightweight documentation, easier prioritization, focus on behaviour rather than implementation, and facilitating conversation between stakeholders. The "we'll deliver View Inventory in two weeks" and "email notification for low ratings" examples are the lesson's official examples, so know them well.

### Remember This
A user story is less valuable as a static sentence and more valuable as the start of a useful conversation.

## Concept 5: SMART User Stories

### Simple Definition
**SMART** is a set of five characteristics that good user stories should have: **Specific**, **Measurable**, **Achievable**, **Relevant**, and **Time-boxed**. This is one of the most heavily tested topics in this lesson.

### Why It Exists
The SMART checklist exists because not every short, informal sentence automatically makes a good user story. Without a clear standard to check against, teams might write vague, untestable, or oversized user stories that cause just as much confusion as a poorly written SRS requirement would. SMART gives teams a clear, repeatable checklist to make sure every user story is actually useful and workable.

### Why It Matters to You
If you write user stories without following SMART, you risk creating stories that are too vague to implement correctly, impossible to test, too large to finish in one sprint, lacking real business value, or missing a clear deadline. Each of these problems can cause just as much wasted time and miscommunication as the very problems user stories were meant to solve in the first place.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about setting a personal goal. If you say "I want to get fit," that goal is vague, hard to measure, and has no real deadline — it is almost impossible to know if you have succeeded or failed. Compare this to "I want to be able to run five kilometers without stopping within eight weeks." This second goal is specific (running five kilometers), measurable (without stopping), achievable (a realistic distance to build up to), relevant (assuming fitness matters to you), and time-boxed (within eight weeks). SMART user stories work exactly the same way — they turn vague intentions into clear, checkable, workable goals.

### How It Works — Step by Step

1. **Specific** — make sure the user story clearly defines exactly what should be implemented, with no room for multiple interpretations. For example, "users can search for a product in the catalog" is vague, since it does not specify by what attribute. A more specific version is "the user can search for a product by title in the catalog."
2. **Measurable** — make sure the user story can be tested using measurable, expected results for given inputs. For example, "the seller portal should have good response time" is vague and hard to measure. A more measurable version is "when adding a product to the catalog, the product should appear in the catalog within three seconds."
3. **Achievable** — make sure the user story can realistically be completed within one agile sprint, typically one to two weeks. For example, "view inventory" might be too large to complete in one sprint, so it can be divided into smaller sub-stories, such as first implementing a simple page that views all products, and later adding pagination and filters as a separate story.
4. **Relevant** — make sure the user story provides real business value to one or more stakeholders. To check this, ask questions like "why is this feature needed?" and "is it really important, and is it adding any value?" to confirm the story is genuinely worth building.
5. **Time-boxed** — make sure the user story has a clear time budget, and stop implementing it once that time budget has been exceeded. If a story runs over its time budget, the team can either give up on it, divide it into smaller stories, or reschedule the remaining work based on a revised estimate.

### Real-World Example
Think about the **Amazon Seller Portal** again. A poorly written user story might say, "Users can increase cross-selling," which is vague (not specific), has no way to measure success (not measurable), and lacks any real deadline (not time-boxed). A SMART version would instead say, "As a seller, I want the system to recommend related products on a product page, increasing add-to-cart actions by at least ten percent, so that I can boost sales, to be completed within two weeks." This is an example of SMART user stories because the second version fixes the vague, unmeasurable, and undeadlined version into something specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-boxed.

### Common Mistake
You might think SMART only applies to writing the user story sentence itself, like getting the wording right. In reality, SMART is also about practical project management — for example, the Achievable criterion is really about correctly sizing work for a sprint, and the Time-boxed criterion is about actively managing a story that runs over budget, not just writing a deadline into the sentence and forgetting about it.

### Exam Tip
This is explicitly flagged as one of the most tested topics, so be ready to identify which SMART letter is "missing" from a poorly written user story, using this quick decision rule: missing numbers or metrics points to Measurable, a missing deadline points to Time-boxed, vague or broad wording points to Specific, a story too big for a sprint points to Achievable, and no clear business value points to Relevant.

### Remember This
SMART turns a vague wish into a workable plan: clear, testable, sized right, valuable, and on a deadline.

## SMART Exam Alert — Quick Decision Tree

This shortcut is explicitly highlighted as appearing in nearly every exam paper on this topic, so memorize it carefully.

- Missing numbers or metrics in the story? → **Measurable** is the missing criterion.
- Missing a deadline or timeframe? → **Time-boxed** is the missing criterion.
- Story is too vague or broad? → **Specific** is the missing criterion.
- Story is too big to finish in one sprint? → **Achievable** is the missing criterion.
- No clear business value or reason given? → **Relevant** is the missing criterion.

If a story exceeds its time budget, remember the three options: give up on the story, divide it into smaller stories, or reschedule the remaining work according to a revised estimate.

## Concept 6: Drawbacks of User Stories

### Simple Definition
The **drawbacks of user stories** are the practical situations where this lightweight, agile approach does not work as well, particularly when continuous customer contact is difficult, or when projects are very large or safety-critical.

### Why It Exists
This concept exists because user stories, like the SRS, are not a perfect solution for every single project. Understanding their limitations honestly helps teams choose the right approach for the right situation, rather than assuming agile and user stories are always the best choice no matter what kind of software is being built.

### Why It Matters to You
If you assume user stories work well for every project, you risk choosing the wrong approach for a project where it genuinely does not fit, such as a safety-critical system where errors cannot be tolerated and extensive upfront planning is legally or practically required. Recognizing these limitations helps you make a more informed choice between the plan and document model with its SRS, and the agile model with its user stories.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about building a simple backyard treehouse versus building an airplane. For the treehouse, you can adjust your plan as you go, asking your family for feedback along the way, much like an agile approach with user stories. But for an airplane, you cannot simply "adjust as you go" once it is flying — every single detail must be planned, documented, and verified extensively before it is ever built, because mistakes can be catastrophic. This is exactly why safety-critical software, much like the airplane, often cannot rely on the lightweight, continuously evolving approach that user stories represent.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Recognize that user stories depend on **continuous customer involvement** throughout development, since conversations and feedback are central to refining requirements.
2. Recognize that this continuous involvement may be **difficult or expensive** to maintain, because customers may not want to participate constantly, or may not be available at all times throughout the project.
3. Recognize that the user story approach **may not scale well** to very large software development projects, where the sheer number and complexity of features makes a lightweight, conversation-driven approach harder to manage.
4. Recognize that **safety-critical applications**, where there is no room for errors or late refinement, typically require extensive planning and documentation before implementation even begins, which does not fit well with the flexible, evolving nature of user stories.
5. Remember the key overall point: the right software development methodology depends on the type of application you are building, not on which approach is generally considered more "modern" or "popular."

### Real-World Example
Think about an **online banking** system's core transaction processing software, which is safety-critical in the sense that any error could cause serious financial harm to customers. A bank would likely rely heavily on detailed upfront planning and documentation, similar to a full SRS, rather than purely lightweight, evolving user stories, because there is very little room for errors or late-stage changes in how money is transferred and recorded. This is an example of a drawback of user stories because it shows a real situation — a safety-critical financial system — where the continuously evolving, lightweight agile approach is not the best fit.

### Common Mistake
You might think that because agile and user stories are widely used and popular, they must always be the better choice compared to a heavier, document-driven approach like the SRS. This is incorrect. The lesson's key takeaway is that the right methodology depends entirely on the type of application being built — stable, well-understood, or safety-critical projects may still be better served by a more document-heavy approach.

### Exam Tip
Be ready to list the specific drawbacks: difficulty maintaining continuous customer contact, poor scalability to very large projects, and unsuitability for safety-critical applications requiring extensive documentation. Exams may give you a project scenario and ask you to judge whether user stories or a traditional SRS approach would fit better.

### Remember This
User stories are great when you can adjust as you go — but for safety-critical systems, you cannot just "adjust as you go" after it is already built.

## Key Takeaways

- Traditional SRS documents struggle when customer requirements are unsure or constantly evolving, which is the core problem that motivates the agile perspective.
- Behaviour Driven Design (BDD) asks questions about an application's behaviour before and during development, continuously refining requirements to reduce miscommunication.
- User stories are the BDD version of requirements, replacing the SRS with short, informal, plain-language descriptions written using the Role-Feature-Benefit template: "As a [role], I want [feature], so that [benefit]."
- User stories are the smallest unit of work that can be completed in one sprint, typically one to two weeks, and originate from the human-computer interaction community.
- The benefits of user stories include lightweight documentation, easier prioritization, a focus on behaviour rather than implementation, and facilitating valuable conversations between stakeholders.
- Good user stories should be SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-boxed, and this is one of the most heavily tested topics in this lesson.
- User stories have real drawbacks, including the need for continuous customer involvement, poor scalability to very large projects, and unsuitability for safety-critical applications.
- The right requirements methodology — SRS or user stories — depends on the type of application being built, not on which approach is more modern or popular.

## Exam Essentials

### Important Definitions
- **Behaviour Driven Design (BDD)** — an agile approach that asks questions about an application's behaviour before and during development, continuously refining requirements to reduce miscommunication.
- **User Story** — a short, informal, plain-language description of what a user wants to do within a software product, highlighting the value it provides, and representing the smallest unit of work completed in one sprint.
- **Sprint** — a short development cycle in agile, typically one to two weeks long.
- **Role-Feature-Benefit Template** — the standard format for writing user stories: "As a [role], I want [feature], so that [benefit]."
- **SMART** — a checklist for good user stories: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-boxed.

### Potential MCQs

**1. What is the core problem that motivates the move from SRS to Behaviour Driven Design?**
A) SRS documents are too short to be useful
B) Customers are unsure of their requirements, and these requirements evolve over time ✔
C) SRS documents cannot describe non-functional requirements
D) Developers prefer not to write any documentation at all

The correct answer is that customers are often unsure of their requirements, and their understanding evolves over time, making a fixed SRS hard to maintain. SRS documents are not too short; in fact, the previous lesson described them as quite detailed and heavy. SRS documents can describe non-functional requirements, as covered in earlier lessons. Developers in BDD still write documentation, just in a lighter form, so refusing to document at all is not the reason for this shift.

**2. Which of the following correctly follows the Role-Feature-Benefit user story template?**
A) "The system must support 500 simultaneous logins"
B) "As an independent seller, I want to view my inventory, so that I can identify low-stock products" ✔
C) "Add, edit, and delete catalog items"
D) "The product must appear in the catalog within five seconds"

The correct answer clearly follows the "As a [role], I want [feature], so that [benefit]" structure, naming the role (independent seller), the feature (view inventory), and the benefit (identifying low-stock products). The first option is a non-functional requirement written in SRS style, not a user story. The third option is a bare functional requirement without the role-feature-benefit structure. The fourth option is also a non-functional requirement, lacking the user story template entirely.

**3. A user story says, "The app should increase cross-selling," without specifying by how much. Which SMART criterion is missing?**
A) Specific
B) Measurable ✔
C) Achievable
D) Relevant

Measurable is correct, since the story lacks any quantifiable metric, such as a specific percentage increase, making it untestable. It is not a Specific problem, since the feature itself (cross-selling) is reasonably clear; it is the lack of a measurable target that is the issue. It is not an Achievable problem, since there is no indication the story is too large for a sprint. It is not a Relevant problem either, since increasing cross-selling could plausibly have business value; the issue here is purely the missing metric.

**4. According to the lesson, what should a team do if a user story exceeds its time budget?**
A) Always cancel the entire project
B) Give up on the story, divide it into smaller stories, or reschedule the remaining work ✔
C) Ignore the time budget and continue as planned
D) Convert the user story into a full SRS document

Giving up, dividing the story, or rescheduling is correct, since these are the three specific options the lesson gives for handling a story that has exceeded its time-boxed budget. Cancelling the entire project is an extreme and unsupported response to one overrunning story. Ignoring the time budget defeats the purpose of having a time-boxed criterion at all. Converting the story into a full SRS document is not mentioned anywhere in the lesson as a response to a missed deadline.

**5. Which type of project is explicitly mentioned as a poor fit for the user story approach?**
A) A small mobile app with frequent user feedback
B) A safety-critical application requiring extensive documentation before implementation ✔
C) A startup building its first prototype
D) A feature update for an existing well-understood app

Safety-critical applications are correct, since the lesson explicitly states that such applications require extensive planning and documentation before implementation, leaving no room for errors or late refinement. Small mobile apps with frequent feedback are actually a good fit for user stories, not a poor one. Startups building first prototypes commonly use agile and user stories effectively. Feature updates to well-understood apps are also generally well suited to the lightweight, iterative nature of user stories.

## Quick Memory Tricks

- **BDD vs SRS:** Think "ask again and again" — BDD keeps asking about behaviour throughout development, instead of answering once and freezing the answer like an SRS.
- **User Story Template:** Remember "Who, What, Why" — As a [Who], I want [What], so that [Why it matters].
- **SMART:** Remember "Clear, Countable, Doable, Worthwhile, Deadlined" as a plain-English version of Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-boxed.
- **Drawbacks of User Stories:** Picture an airplane versus a treehouse — you can adjust a treehouse as you build it, but an airplane needs everything planned and documented before it ever takes off.

## Quick Recap — How This Connects to Earlier Lessons

This lesson builds directly on what you already studied. Functional requirements describe WHAT a system should do, and non-functional requirements describe HOW WELL it should behave — both ideas still apply inside user stories, since a story's "feature" is often functional, while SMART's "Measurable" criterion often adds a non-functional-style metric, like a specific number of seconds or a percentage. The SRS document you studied in the previous lesson is the plan-and-document equivalent of what user stories represent in the agile world — both are ways of organizing requirements, just suited to very different situations.

## One-Line Lesson Summary

Behaviour Driven Design replaces the heavy, fixed SRS document with short, plain-language user stories written in the Role-Feature-Benefit format, which should always be SMART — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-boxed — though this lightweight approach still has real limits in very large or safety-critical projects.
