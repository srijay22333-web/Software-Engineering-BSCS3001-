# Week 2 — Lesson 2: Identifying Users & Requirements

## Lesson Overview

Welcome back! In the last lesson, you learned that software teams must identify primary, secondary, and tertiary users before building anything. Here is the simple version of what comes next: now that you know WHO your users are, this lesson teaches you HOW to actually get information out of them. Think of it like being a detective — you already know who your witnesses are, but now you need different techniques to get the truth out of each one, because some witnesses talk easily, some need careful questioning, and some reveal more through their actions than their words. This is actually easier than it sounds, because you will learn five practical techniques — questionnaires, interviews, focus groups, observation, and documentation — using the same Amazon Seller Portal example from before. By the end of this lesson, you will know exactly which technique to use, and when, to get the most accurate picture of what your users really need.

## Concept 1: Questionnaires

### Simple Definition
A **questionnaire** is a series of questions that are designed to pull out specific information from users. You have probably filled one out before, like a feedback form after a course or a survey about a product.

### Why It Exists
Questionnaires exist because, in many real projects, you cannot sit down and personally talk to every single user. Your users might be spread across many different cities or even countries, and talking to each one individually would take far too long. Questionnaires were created so that one set of questions could be sent out to a large number of people at once, no matter where they are located, and answers could still be collected efficiently.

### Why It Matters to You
If you skip using questionnaires when you have a large or spread-out user base, you will end up either spending huge amounts of time interviewing each person individually, or you will only get requirements from a small handful of users you managed to reach. This means your software will be designed around the needs of just a few people, while ignoring the needs of everyone else, which is a serious gap when your final product needs to work for thousands of users.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Imagine your school wants to know what flavor of ice cream to serve at the next event, but there are 2,000 students spread across different classes and even different buildings. It would be almost impossible for the event team to personally ask all 2,000 students one by one. Instead, they hand out a simple slip of paper to every student with a few flavor options to circle, or a blank line to write in their own suggestion. This slip of paper is exactly like a questionnaire — it lets the team collect specific information (favorite flavor) from a huge, spread-out group, quickly and efficiently, which maps directly to how questionnaires work for gathering software requirements.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Design a set of specific questions that you want answered, keeping in mind exactly what information you are trying to learn.
2. Decide the type of question for each item — it could be a simple yes or no question, a multiple-choice question where the user picks from a set of answers, or an open-ended question where the user writes a longer response or comment.
3. Send the questionnaire out to your target group of users, even if they are spread across a wide geographical area, since questionnaires do not require face-to-face contact.
4. Collect the responses once users have filled them in.
5. Analyze the answers to find patterns, such as common difficulties or common needs, which become candidate requirements for your software.
6. Always combine this technique with other requirement-gathering techniques, since a questionnaire alone gives you only the specific answers you asked for, and nothing more.

### Real-World Example
Think about **YouTube**. Suppose YouTube wants to know how creators across the world feel about their current analytics dashboard. Instead of personally calling thousands of creators, YouTube sends out a questionnaire asking specific questions, such as "On a scale of 1 to 5, how useful do you find the current analytics dashboard?" or "Which metric do you check most often?" Because creators live all over the world, a questionnaire lets YouTube gather consistent, specific answers from a huge number of people very quickly. This is an example of a questionnaire because YouTube is using a fixed set of designed questions to pull specific information from a large, geographically spread-out group of users.

### Common Mistake
You might think that a questionnaire alone is enough to fully understand what users need, since it can reach so many people at once. This is a mistake, because questionnaires only give you answers to the exact questions you thought to ask — they cannot reveal a need you never thought of asking about. This is exactly why the lesson stresses that questionnaires must always be used together with other techniques like interviews or observation.

### Exam Tip
The exam often tests whether you can tell apart the three types of questionnaire questions — yes/no, multiple-choice, and open-ended — and whether you understand when questionnaires are the best choice (a large, spread-out group needing specific, often quantitative, answers). Be ready to give your own example question for each question type.

### Remember This
Questionnaires are best when you have many users, far apart, and specific questions you already know you want answered.

## Concept 2: Interviews

### Simple Definition
An **interview** is a requirement-gathering technique where someone asks a person a set of questions directly, usually face-to-face, but it can also be done over the phone or online.

### Why It Exists
Interviews exist because questionnaires alone only collect answers to questions you already thought to ask. Sometimes, a requirement-gathering team does not yet know enough about a user's situation to even write good questionnaire questions. Interviews were developed to fill this gap, allowing a team to start with broad, open questions and then dig deeper based on what the person actually says, uncovering things the team never would have known to ask about in advance.

### Why It Matters to You
If you only ever interview people instead of using other techniques too, you will end up designing software based on just one person's perspective at a time, since each interview reflects only that individual's experience. This matters because real systems need to serve many different kinds of users, and relying only on interviews risks building a system that fits one person's story well but completely misses other important needs.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about visiting a doctor for the first time. The doctor does not hand you a multiple-choice form and walk away — instead, they sit with you and ask open questions like "what's been bothering you?" Based on your answer, they ask follow-up questions, like "when did this start?" or "does it get worse at night?" The doctor is probing deeper based on what you actually say, rather than sticking to a fixed list of questions written in advance. This is exactly how an interview works in software requirement gathering — the interviewer starts broad, listens carefully, and then probes deeper into specific issues that come up, instead of just collecting fixed answers like a questionnaire does.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Decide on the interview style: a **structured** interview uses a fixed set of questions that the interviewer asks in order, without much deviation. An **unstructured** interview has no fixed questions at all, because the interviewer does not yet know exactly what to ask, so they explore broadly based on the conversation. A **semi-structured** interview sits in between, with some planned questions but room to explore further.
2. Begin with broad, open-ended questions, especially if you do not yet know much about the user's situation. For example, ask independent sellers what platforms they currently use to sell their products.
3. Listen carefully to the answers given. For example, a seller might answer that they use WhatsApp and Instagram to sell products.
4. Probe deeper into specific points the user raises, asking follow-up questions about advantages and disadvantages of what they mentioned.
5. Pay attention to specific pain points that come up during probing, since these often reveal real requirements. For example, a seller might mention that tracking orders, tracking payments, tracking inventory, and choosing selling options are all big overheads for them.
6. Use these revealed pain points to identify concrete software requirements, such as the need for order tracking or payment tracking features.
7. Remember that interviews are especially useful early in the requirement-gathering process, when you are still trying to discover what scenarios and issues even exist.

### Real-World Example
Think about **Instagram**. Suppose Instagram's product team wants to improve their "Reels" editing tools. They could interview a handful of content creators with an open question like "walk me through how you currently edit your Reels." A creator might mention they switch between three different apps just to add captions, trim clips, and add music, because Instagram's built-in tools feel limited. The interviewer can then probe further, asking which specific step takes the most time. This deeper digging reveals a concrete requirement: creators need a faster, all-in-one editing flow inside the app itself. This is an example of an interview because Instagram's team is having a direct, probing conversation with individual users to uncover specific needs that a simple questionnaire would likely have missed.

### Common Mistake
You might think interviews give you a complete and balanced picture of what all users need, simply because the conversation feels detailed and rich. This is incorrect, because each interview only reflects the perspective of the one person you spoke to. The lesson is clear that this is a real drawback of interviews — they offer only one perspective at a time, which is exactly why teams also use focus groups to hear multiple viewpoints together.

### Exam Tip
Expect the exam to test the three types of interviews — structured, unstructured, and semi-structured — and ask you to explain when each would be used. Also expect questions comparing interviews to questionnaires (interviews are better for unknown or qualitative issues, questionnaires are better for known, specific, quantitative questions across large groups).

### Remember This
Interviews start broad and dig deep, but remember — one interview only ever shows you one person's side of the story.

## Concept 3: Focus Groups

### Simple Definition
A **focus group** is a requirement-gathering technique where a group of stakeholders is brought together to discuss issues and requirements together, rather than speaking to each person separately.

### Why It Exists
Focus groups exist to solve the exact weakness of interviews mentioned above — the fact that one interview only gives one person's perspective. By bringing multiple stakeholders into the same room or call, a team can immediately see whether different people share the same concerns, or whether their needs actually conflict with each other, which a single one-on-one interview could never reveal on its own.

### Why It Matters to You
If you rely only on individual interviews and skip focus groups, you might walk away thinking a particular requirement is important, simply because one person mentioned it strongly, without realizing that other stakeholders might disagree or have a completely different priority. Focus groups matter because they let you see agreement and disagreement happening live, which helps you decide which requirements are truly shared, important needs versus which are just one person's personal preference.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about planning a class trip destination. If you ask just one classmate where they want to go, they might say "the beach," and you might assume everyone wants that. But if you instead gather a group of classmates together and ask them all at once, you might discover that while three classmates love the beach idea, two others strongly prefer a hiking trip instead, and a heated discussion reveals exactly why each group feels that way. This group discussion gives you a much richer, more honest picture of what the whole class actually wants and where the real disagreements lie, compared to asking just one person. This maps directly onto focus groups in software requirement gathering, where bringing stakeholders together reveals both shared agreement and real conflicts.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Identify a group of stakeholders who represent different perspectives relevant to the system being built. For example, gather sales team managers from several different verticals, such as mobile sales and furniture sales.
2. Bring this group together in one session, either in person or online, to discuss issues and requirements as a group rather than one at a time.
3. Ask the group open discussion questions and let participants respond to each other, not just to the interviewer.
4. Watch for moments of **consensus**, where multiple stakeholders independently raise the same concern — this is a strong signal that the issue is a genuine, important requirement.
5. Watch for moments of **disagreement**, where different stakeholders want different things. For example, a mobile sales team might be very interested in seeing the sales summary right at the launch of a new product, while a furniture sales team might care more about a sales summary covering the last 30 days.
6. Document both the points of agreement and the points of disagreement, since both are useful — agreement confirms shared requirements, while disagreement reveals that the system might need flexible features, like letting users choose a custom date range for sales summaries (for example, last X days or a specific day).

### Real-World Example
Think about **Netflix**. Suppose Netflix gathers a focus group made up of parents, teenagers, and elderly viewers together to discuss their experience with parental controls and content recommendations. The parents might strongly agree that they want easier ways to restrict mature content for their children — this is consensus, showing a real shared requirement. At the same time, teenagers in the group might push back, saying restrictions feel too strict, while elderly viewers might say they barely use any filters at all — this is disagreement, showing that different age groups have very different needs. This is an example of a focus group because Netflix is bringing multiple types of stakeholders together in one session to reveal both shared concerns and real conflicts that a single interview with just one parent would never have shown.

### Common Mistake
You might think a focus group is just multiple interviews happening in the same room. This is incorrect, because the real value of a focus group comes specifically from the interaction between participants — people responding to and challenging each other's points — not just from collecting more individual answers in one place. If participants never interact with each other's opinions, you lose the main benefit of using a focus group at all.

### Exam Tip
The exam often asks you to compare interviews and focus groups directly. Be ready to clearly state that interviews give deep, individual perspective but only one viewpoint, while focus groups reveal consensus and conflict across multiple stakeholders at once. The mobile-versus-furniture sales summary example is the lesson's official example, so know it well.

### Remember This
One interview tells you what one person thinks; one focus group tells you whether everyone agrees — or exactly where they don't.

## Concept 4: Naturalistic Observation

### Simple Definition
**Naturalistic observation** is a requirement-gathering technique where you spend time watching stakeholders perform their everyday tasks in their normal environment, instead of just asking them questions.

### Why It Exists
This technique exists because of a simple but important truth: what people say they do is often different from what they actually do. Humans frequently find it difficult to accurately describe their own actions and habits in words, even when they are being completely honest. Naturalistic observation was developed to capture the real, true picture of how people behave, by watching them directly rather than relying only on what they report about themselves.

### Why It Matters to You
If you only ever ask users about their behavior through questionnaires or interviews, you risk building software based on an inaccurate or incomplete description of what people actually do. This matters because the gap between what people say and what they do can lead you to miss important requirements entirely — requirements that only become obvious once you watch someone struggle with a task in real life.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Think about asking a friend, "How exactly do you make tea every morning?" They might say something simple like "I just boil water and add a tea bag." But if you actually watched them one morning, you might notice they always check their phone while waiting, always add exactly two spoons of sugar without measuring, and always let it sit for precisely two minutes because they timed it once and remembered. None of these small but important details came out when you simply asked them — they only appeared because you watched them do it. This is exactly how naturalistic observation works for gathering software requirements: by watching real behavior, you catch details that would never come up in a simple conversation.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Choose a stakeholder whose day-to-day tasks are relevant to the system you are building.
2. Spend time directly with that stakeholder while they go about their normal work, in their natural setting, rather than in an artificial interview room.
3. Shadow the stakeholder, which means following along closely and watching what they actually do, step by step.
4. Make notes occasionally about specific actions, habits, or workarounds you observe.
5. Ask the stakeholder questions only when needed for clarification, but keep the main focus on observing their actual behavior rather than on conversation.
6. Translate what you observe into concrete software requirements. For example, observing how shopkeepers give product recommendations to customers in a physical shop, or observing how shop owners value getting timely feedback about a product, can directly translate into online requirements like a recommendation feature or a timely customer feedback system.

### Real-World Example
Think about **Uber**. Suppose Uber's team wants to improve the driver app experience. Instead of just asking drivers in an interview how they use the app, the team could ride along with a driver for a full shift and observe directly. They might notice that the driver glances at the map constantly while also trying to read incoming messages from riders, creating a real safety risk and inefficiency that the driver might never have mentioned in words, simply because it felt like a normal part of the job to them. This observation could lead to a real requirement, such as a voice-read-aloud feature for rider messages. This is an example of naturalistic observation because Uber's team is directly watching real driver behavior in its natural setting to discover a requirement that conversation alone might have missed.

### Common Mistake
You might think naturalistic observation is just a backup technique used only when you cannot get an interview. This is incorrect, because observation serves a completely different and valuable purpose — it captures real behavior that people often cannot accurately describe in words, even when they are trying their best to be honest and clear. It is not a lesser substitute for an interview; it reveals a different category of requirement entirely.

### Exam Tip
The exam may test why observation is necessary even when interviews and questionnaires already exist. Be ready to explain the core reason clearly: what people say and what people actually do are often different, and observation is the only technique here that captures real behavior directly rather than self-reported behavior.

### Remember This
People often cannot accurately describe what they do — so sometimes, the only way to truly know is to watch.

## Concept 5: Documentation

### Simple Definition
**Documentation**, as a requirement-gathering technique, means studying existing manuals, procedures, rules, and regulations that are relevant to the tasks your software needs to support.

### Why It Exists
This technique exists because many important rules and constraints already exist in written form, long before any requirement-gathering interview or questionnaire even happens. Banking rules, legal regulations, and standard operating procedures are often written down by organizations outside of your direct stakeholders, and these rules can have a major impact on what your software is allowed to do. Documentation review exists to make sure these existing rules are not missed simply because no single interviewed stakeholder happened to mention them.

### Why It Matters to You
If you skip studying existing documentation, you risk building a software feature that directly violates an existing rule or regulation, which can cause serious legal or operational problems later. For example, if you do not check banking regulations, you might design a payment feature for sellers that the actual banking system is not legally allowed to support, forcing a costly redesign after the fact.

### Intuition — Understand It Without Jargon
Imagine you are building a new clubhouse for your neighborhood, and before laying a single brick, you check the local council's building rules booklet to see how tall the fence can be, where the clubhouse can be placed on the land, and what safety codes must be followed. You did not need to ask any neighbor about these rules, because they were already written down by the local council long before you started planning. This is exactly what documentation review does in software requirement gathering — instead of asking every stakeholder, you go directly to the existing written rules, like bank regulations, to understand constraints that already exist.

### How It Works — Step by Step
1. Identify which existing documents are relevant to the system you are building, such as manuals, standard procedures, or regulations.
2. Locate and review these documents directly, rather than relying on stakeholders to remember and explain every rule from memory.
3. Extract specific procedures, steps, or regulations that will affect how your software must be designed. For example, for the Amazon Seller Portal, bank regulations are highly relevant, since they govern things like how seller bank accounts can be added to the portal, and how frequently money can be deposited into seller accounts.
4. Cross-check these documented rules and procedures against the requirements gathered from other techniques, like interviews or questionnaires, to spot constraints that stakeholders may not have mentioned themselves.
5. Build these documented constraints directly into your list of system requirements, since they represent rules the software must follow regardless of what any individual stakeholder personally wants.

### Real-World Example
Think about an **online banking** app. Before building a feature that lets customers transfer money internationally, the development team must review existing regulatory documentation, such as anti-money-laundering rules and foreign exchange limits set by financial authorities. No customer in an interview would think to mention these specific legal limits, since customers usually do not memorize banking law. This is an example of documentation as a requirement-gathering technique because the team is pulling crucial, binding requirements directly from existing regulatory documents rather than from any stakeholder conversation.

### Common Mistake
You might think documentation review is less important than talking to real users, since it does not involve any human interaction at all. This is incorrect, because documentation often reveals hard constraints, like legal regulations, that stakeholders may not even know about or may forget to mention, but that the software absolutely must follow regardless.

### Exam Tip
The exam likely tests this with the specific bank regulation example from the Amazon Seller Portal — adding seller bank accounts and deposit frequency. Be ready to explain why documentation review is necessary even when you have already interviewed stakeholders directly.

### Remember This
Some rules are not in anyone's head — they are written down, and documentation review is how you find them.

## Key Takeaways

- Now that primary, secondary, and tertiary users have been identified, the next step is choosing the right technique to actually gather requirements from each of them.
- Questionnaires are best for collecting specific, often quantitative answers from a large group of users spread across a wide area, but should always be combined with other techniques.
- Interviews are useful early in requirement gathering for exploring unknown issues deeply, using structured, unstructured, or semi-structured formats, but they only reflect one person's perspective.
- Focus groups bring multiple stakeholders together at once, revealing both consensus (shared important requirements) and disagreement (conflicting needs) that a single interview cannot show.
- Naturalistic observation involves watching stakeholders perform real tasks in their natural setting, because what people say they do is often different from what they actually do.
- Documentation review means studying existing manuals, procedures, and regulations, such as bank rules, which can reveal binding requirements that no stakeholder interview would surface.
- For the Amazon Seller Portal, these techniques together identified requirements such as managing the catalog and inventory, and tracking orders, payments, sales, and customer feedback.
- Effective requirement gathering involves engaging all stakeholder groups, combining multiple techniques, running a pilot session to test your methods first, and being pragmatic since data gathering is expensive and time-consuming.

## Exam Essentials

### Important Definitions
- **Questionnaire** — a series of questions designed to elicit specific information from users, useful for large or geographically spread-out groups.
- **Interview** — a requirement-gathering technique involving asking a person a set of questions directly, which can be structured, unstructured, or semi-structured.
- **Structured Interview** — an interview that follows a fixed set of prepared questions asked in order.
- **Unstructured Interview** — an interview with no fixed set of questions, where the interviewer explores broadly because they do not yet know exactly what to ask.
- **Focus Group** — a session where a group of stakeholders discusses issues and requirements together, revealing consensus and disagreement.
- **Naturalistic Observation** — a technique involving watching stakeholders perform their everyday tasks in their normal environment, to capture real behavior rather than self-reported behavior.
- **Documentation (as a technique)** — reviewing existing manuals, procedures, and regulations relevant to the system being built.
- **Pilot Session** — a small test run of a requirement-gathering method, such as a questionnaire, done before the full-scale gathering effort, to catch mistakes or missing options.

### Potential MCQs

**1. Which requirement-gathering technique is best suited for collecting answers to specific, already-known questions from a large, geographically spread-out group?**
A) Interviews
B) Focus groups
C) Questionnaires ✔
D) Naturalistic observation

Interviews are wrong because they are better suited to deep, individual exploration rather than reaching large spread-out groups efficiently. Focus groups are wrong because they require gathering people together, which works against a widely spread-out audience. Naturalistic observation is wrong because it requires direct, time-consuming, one-on-one shadowing, not mass data collection.

**2. In an interview where the interviewer does not yet know what specific questions to ask because the user's needs are unclear, which type of interview is being used?**
A) Structured interview
B) Unstructured interview ✔
C) Focus group interview
D) Documented interview

Unstructured interview is correct because the lesson defines this type as one where the interviewer does not know what questions to ask in advance and instead explores broadly. A structured interview is wrong because it uses a fixed, prepared set of questions, which contradicts not knowing what to ask. "Focus group interview" is wrong because a focus group involves multiple stakeholders discussing together, not a single interviewer-and-user format. "Documented interview" is not a real term used in this lesson at all.

**3. What is the main advantage of using a focus group instead of only individual interviews?**
A) It takes less time than an interview
B) It reveals consensus and disagreement among multiple stakeholders at once ✔
C) It removes the need for any further requirement gathering
D) It only works for primary users

Revealing consensus and disagreement is correct because the lesson explicitly states this as the key advantage of focus groups over single-perspective interviews. Taking less time is incorrect, since organizing and running a group discussion can actually take significant coordination and time. Removing the need for further requirement gathering is incorrect, since the lesson stresses combining multiple techniques, not replacing them all with one. Focus groups are not limited to only primary users; they can include any relevant stakeholder group.

**4. Why is naturalistic observation considered a valuable requirement-gathering technique?**
A) It is faster than questionnaires
B) It avoids the need to identify stakeholders
C) People often cannot accurately describe what they actually do, so direct observation reveals the truth ✔
D) It replaces the need for documentation review

The correct answer is that people often cannot accurately describe their own actions, so watching them directly reveals requirements that conversation alone would miss. Naturalistic observation is not faster than questionnaires, since it requires spending real time shadowing someone. It does not avoid identifying stakeholders, since you must still choose a relevant stakeholder to observe. It also does not replace documentation review, since both techniques serve very different purposes and are meant to be combined.

**5. In the Amazon Seller Portal example, which requirement-gathering technique revealed the need to understand bank regulations for adding seller accounts and deposit frequency?**
A) Questionnaires
B) Focus groups
C) Naturalistic observation
D) Documentation ✔

Documentation is correct because the lesson specifically uses bank regulations on adding seller accounts and deposit frequency as its example of reviewing existing documentation. Questionnaires are incorrect because they involve asking users direct questions, not reviewing written regulatory documents. Focus groups are incorrect because they involve group discussion among stakeholders, not document review. Naturalistic observation is incorrect because it involves watching real behavior, not reading regulations.

## Quick Memory Tricks

- **Questionnaires:** Think "wide net, fixed questions" — like throwing one big fishing net with the same holes everywhere, catching the same type of specific answer from a huge spread-out group all at once.
- **Interviews:** Picture a detective starting with "tell me what happened" and then zooming in closer and closer with follow-up questions — broad first, narrow later, but always just one witness's story.
- **Focus Groups:** Imagine a group debate where you learn the most not from any single speaker, but from watching who agrees and who argues back.
- **Naturalistic Observation:** Remember "actions speak louder than answers" — sometimes the only way to know the truth is to quietly watch, not ask.
- **Documentation:** Think of it as "rules written before you arrived" — like reading the rulebook of a board game instead of asking other players, who might not even remember every rule correctly.

## One-Line Lesson Summary

To gather accurate requirements from primary, secondary, and tertiary users, software teams combine questionnaires for broad specific data, interviews for deep individual insight, focus groups for consensus and conflict, naturalistic observation for real behavior, and documentation for existing rules, since no single technique alone can capture the full picture.
